The Problem: Paramedics Excluded from the Data Ecosystem

Paramedics are the first clinical contact in most emergency episodes — yet they arrive on scene with no access to medical history, medications, allergies, or prior 911 interactions. Triage nurses retrieve the same information with a single click.

Patients are forced to retell their story at every step: dispatch → paramedic → triage → physician → specialist. This delays intervention and introduces miscommunication at every handoff point.

Health data today is custodian-centric, not patient-centric. Each organization holds its own version of the patient record — siloed, slightly different, and inaccessible to the others.

The 911 system is burdened by non-emergent calls because patients lack primary care access. Without richer information, paramedics have only one option: transport to an ED that may not be the right destination.

Harm #1 — Breach

Exposing patient data violates privacy and erodes trust in the healthcare system

vs

Harm #2 — Withhold

Withholding patient data forces providers to make decisions without the information they need

Both are serious. The system obsesses over the first while largely tolerating the second. — CPSA, Interoperability Saves Lives (2023)

Expand What Data Access Makes Possible: Beyond the Emergency Department
⌄

Conservative estimates suggest 15–30% of ED visits could be managed in a lower-acuity setting. Data gives paramedics the tools to make that happen — in three concrete directions.

01

Smart Destination Routing

A paramedic who can see that a patient has a documented mental health care plan knows the right destination is a crisis stabilization unit — not the ED. A dialysis patient with a documented access issue routes directly to their unit. Data turns a default transport into an informed clinical decision, for every call.

02

Care Coaching & Pathway Redirection

For low-acuity calls, a paramedic with access to a patient’s record and a telehealth or primary care link can redirect the patient toward the right pathway rather than defaulting to transport. Knowing a patient has a family physician makes the decision not to transport clinically defensible — not just an operational shortcut.

03

Direct Reporting to Primary Care

When a paramedic responds to a call, the patient’s family physician almost never finds out. A structured prehospital encounter report sent automatically to the primary care provider creates a longitudinal picture between physician visits and enables proactive case management for frequent callers.

Together, these three capabilities reframe the paramedic as an intelligent first point of contact in a connected care system — equipped with the information to make the right decision, the policy framework to act on it, and the data infrastructure to close the loop. This is not a futuristic idea. It is a policy and data problem. Both are solvable.

Proposed Solution: A Bidirectional, Patient-Following Data Framework

Data must flow with the patient from the moment a 911 call is initiated — and feedback must flow back to paramedics after care is complete.

1
Dispatch
Patient record opened at first 911 call. History, meds & allergies populated before paramedics arrive on scene.
2
Prehospital
Paramedics access the full record in real time. Vitals, assessments & treatment added and transmitted live.
3
Smart Routing
Enriched data enables direct transport to the right unit — psych, cardiac, ortho — bypassing unnecessary ED processing.
4
Handoff
Receiving team is fully briefed before arrival. Seamless handover replaces the repeated retelling of the patient story.
5
Outcome Loop
Post-discharge summary returned to paramedics. Diagnoses & outcomes close the loop and fuel clinical learning.
06
Why It Will Work

Evidence Base

Community paramedicine with integrated data access shows improved outcomes, reduced ED use, and higher patient satisfaction (Bigham et al., 2013; Dainty et al., 2018).

Aviation Analogy

Aviation coordinates thousands of aircraft globally via a single interoperable standard — mandated by governance, not technology. Healthcare can do the same.

Banking Analogy

Your debit card works at any ATM worldwide through technical standards + regulatory mandates. Patient data can follow patients the same way.

Bidirectional Learning

When paramedics receive outcome feedback — diagnoses, treatments, discharge — their clinical knowledge grows. Closing the loop elevates the entire profession.

Governance-First Design

Prior efforts failed by deploying technology into fragmented governance. This framework builds policy and legislative alignment before any procurement.

The Moral Imperative

Withholding clinical data from frontline providers is an active, preventable harm. As stewards of health data, we are obligated to act.

Paramedics are the first clinical touch in a patient’s care journey — yet the last to have access to their information.

A healthcare system that withholds data from its frontline providers is a system that accepts preventable harm.

Data must follow the patient. Policy must follow the data. Paramedics must be inside the system — not outside it.

A healthy Canadian healthcare ecosystem is one that includes paramedics — fully, equitably, and by design.
